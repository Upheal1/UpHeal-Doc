\chapter*{Abstract}\label{abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

\markboth{Abstract}{Abstract}

The project introduces UpHeal, an AI-powered, data-driven, and gamified
mental health companion designed to provide accessible, empathetic, and
evidence-based mental health support through a mobile application. By
integrating large language models with emotion classification and
retrieval-augmented generation, the system delivers contextually
relevant therapeutic responses while maintaining user engagement through
gamification elements. The application leverages a Flutter-based
cross-platform frontend, a Python FastAPI backend, Supabase for
authentication and data management, and GPU-powered AI inference on
RunPod.

The aim is to practice applying the full spectrum of software
engineering, artificial intelligence, and user experience design skills
acquired throughout the undergraduate program. This includes
requirements engineering, system architecture, machine learning pipeline
design, backend and frontend development, testing, and evaluation within
a real-world healthcare domain that demands ethical responsibility, data
privacy, and clinical sensitivity.

The thesis is organized as follows:

Chapter 1 gives an introduction about the problem. Then moves to the
necessary background. The chapter moves to the problem definition and
proposed solution in brief.

Chapter 2 represents the literature review with recent publications
related to the project topic. From those, the existing solution comes as
an emerging technology to fill in the gaps. A detailed gap analysis is
presented as well.

Chapter 3 represents the most comprehensive chapter. This moves to the
system design and diagrams (Sequence Diagram, Use Case Diagram,
Functional Flow). The model architecture is then described with
necessary hardware considerations. Finally, security considerations are
explained.

Chapter 4 presents the system implementation and the obtained results.
All curves, charts, and comparisons are shown.

Chapter 5 gives the conclusions and future work. Finally, the adopted
ethics considerations are explained in brief.

\chapter{Introduction}\label{sec-introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}\label{sec-background}

Mental health disorders represent one of the leading causes of
disability worldwide. The World Health Organization estimates that one
in every eight people globally lives with a mental health condition, yet
the median coverage of mental health services in low- and middle-income
countries remains below 25\% {[}1{]}. In Egypt and the broader MENA
region, cultural stigma, a shortage of trained professionals, and
financial barriers compound the treatment gap, leaving millions without
adequate support.

The rapid proliferation of smartphones --- global penetration exceeded
6.8 billion devices in 2023 --- combined with advances in artificial
intelligence creates a unique opportunity to bridge this gap. Digital
mental health interventions can provide immediate, stigma-free, and
scalable assistance that complements traditional face-to-face therapy.
Over the past decade, the field has evolved from simple self-help
journals and static psychoeducation modules to sophisticated platforms
that leverage natural language processing, emotion recognition, and
behavioral gamification to deliver personalized support at scale.

Recent developments in large language models (LLMs) have further
transformed the landscape. Models such as LLaMA, Gemma, and GPT-4 have
demonstrated the ability to generate contextually relevant, empathetic
text that can be grounded in curated knowledge bases through
retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) {[}2{]}. When combined with
evidence-based therapeutic frameworks --- Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
(CBT), Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), and structured clinical
assessments such as the Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale
(GAD-7) and the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) --- these models
can produce responses that are not merely conversational but clinically
informed.

Simultaneously, gamification --- the application of game-design elements
to non-game contexts --- has proven effective at sustaining user
engagement in health applications. Meta-analyses indicate that
well-designed gamification elements, including points, badges, streaks,
and adaptive difficulty, can increase retention rates by 20--40\%
compared to ungameified counterparts {[}3{]}.

Despite these advances, a significant gap remains between the promise of
AI-assisted mental health tools and their practical deployment. Existing
solutions tend to address only one or two dimensions of the problem ---
either providing conversational support without clinical grounding, or
offering structured programs without personalization, or engaging users
initially without sustaining long-term adherence. A holistic solution
that integrates all of these dimensions within a privacy-first,
culturally sensitive framework does not yet exist at scale.

\section{Problem Definition}\label{sec-problem-definition}

Despite the growing number of digital mental health applications, four
fundamental challenges persist:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Lack of personalization}. Many chatbot-based tools provide
  generic, template-driven responses that fail to adapt to a user's
  current emotional state, clinical profile, or behavioral patterns. A
  person reporting mild anxiety on Monday and severe anxiety on Thursday
  may receive identical therapeutic suggestions, undermining both trust
  and clinical efficacy.
\item
  \textbf{Low user retention}. Without engaging design, users tend to
  abandon mental health applications within two to four weeks. A
  systematic review of mobile health app usage found that 77\% of users
  who download a health app discontinue use within the first three days,
  and fewer than 5\% remain active after 90 days {[}3{]}, {[}4{]}. The
  absence of progressive reward systems, social accountability, and
  adaptive difficulty curves contributes to this attrition.
\item
  \textbf{Limited clinical grounding}. Few applications integrate
  evidence-based therapeutic frameworks with AI-generated responses.
  Most commercial mental health chatbots operate as closed-domain
  retrieval systems, returning pre-authored scripts from a fixed
  response bank. Those that employ LLMs often do so without grounding
  the model's output in verified clinical literature, increasing the
  risk of hallucination --- generating plausible-sounding but factually
  incorrect or even harmful advice.
\item
  \textbf{Inadequate privacy and safety mechanisms}. Handling sensitive
  mental health data requires robust encryption, access controls, and
  ethical practices. Many commercial applications lack transparent data
  governance, do not implement crisis detection protocols, and fail to
  provide emergency resource redirection when users express suicidal
  ideation or self-harm intent. The absence of culturally localized
  crisis resources further limits the utility of these tools in
  non-Western markets.
\end{enumerate}

These challenges are not independent. Low personalization reduces
perceived value, which accelerates attrition. Attrition starves the
system of the longitudinal data needed to personalize effectively. And
without clinical grounding, even personalized responses can be
dangerous. Addressing these challenges requires an integrated approach.

\section{Proposed Solution}\label{sec-proposed-solution}

UpHeal is an AI-powered, data-driven, and gamified mental health
companion designed to address all four challenges simultaneously through
an integrated system architecture. The system combines four
complementary pillars:

\textbf{Pillar 1: RAG-augmented conversational AI.} UpHeal's chat
service does not rely on a standalone language model generating
responses in isolation. Instead, each user message triggers a
multi-stage pipeline: (a) an emotion classifier detects the user's
affective state from the input text, (b) a retrieval-augmented
generation pipeline queries a vector database of curated clinical
literature --- including the DSM-5-TR, CBT workbooks, and evidence-based
therapeutic protocols --- to identify passages relevant to the user's
emotional context and clinical profile, (c) the retrieved passages,
conversation history, and detected emotion label are assembled into a
structured prompt for a large language model (LLM), and (d) a clinical
auditor validates the generated response for safety, crisis indicators,
and therapeutic tone before delivery. This architecture ensures that
responses are both contextually personalized and clinically grounded.

\textbf{Pillar 2: Real-time emotion-adaptive interaction.} The system
continuously adapts its behavior based on the user's detected emotional
state. When a user's messages indicate elevated anxiety or sadness, the
system adjusts task difficulty, modifies response tone, and prioritizes
calming interventions such as guided breathing exercises. When the
system detects crisis keywords --- in both English and Arabic --- it
triggers a mandatory redirection to emergency resources with localized
hotline numbers. Emotion classification is powered by a fine-tuned model
that processes text input, with future support for voice-based detection
via Whisper speech-to-text transcription.

\textbf{Pillar 3: Comprehensive gamification engine.} UpHeal implements
a multi-layered gamification system designed to sustain engagement over
weeks and months rather than days. The engine includes: an experience
point (XP) system with eight activity types and streak-based multipliers
(up to 5× for 365-day streaks); a leveling system with progressive
thresholds; ten badges across streak, task completion, and
addiction-free categories; twelve achievements with multi-condition
unlock criteria; daily, weekly, and special challenges with time-based
expiration; and animated reward overlays that provide immediate positive
reinforcement. The gamification design draws on self-determination
theory, targeting autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

\textbf{Pillar 4: Privacy-first, safety-augmented architecture.} UpHeal
enforces encryption in transit (TLS 1.3) and at rest (AES-256 via
Supabase), row-level security (RLS) policies that isolate user data, and
a three-tier JWT authentication system (HS256, ES256/JWKS, development
fallback). The clinical auditor operates as a mandatory safety gate:
every generated response is screened for crisis keywords, robotic tone,
safety risks, and frustration indicators before delivery. The auditor
supports bilingual crisis detection (English and Arabic) and provides
locale-specific emergency hotlines.

The system architecture comprises a Flutter cross-platform mobile
application serving as the primary user interface, a Python FastAPI
backend orchestrating the AI pipeline through a microservices gateway,
Supabase providing authentication, PostgreSQL database persistence,
real-time subscriptions, and object storage, a ChromaDB vector store
indexed with sentence-transformer embeddings
(\texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2}, 768 dimensions) housing the clinical
knowledge base, and GPU-hosted inference services for the LLM, emotion
classifier, and speech-to-text components.

\chapter{Literature Review}\label{sec-literature-review}

\section{Overview of Digital Mental Health
Solutions}\label{sec-digital-mh-overview}

The landscape of digital mental health has evolved significantly over
the past decade, moving from simple self-help applications to
sophisticated AI-driven platforms. Early interventions relied primarily
on static content delivery, such as mood logs and psychoeducation
modules, while modern systems leverage natural language processing (NLP)
and machine learning to offer dynamic, personalized support {[}5{]}.

A systematic review by Baumeister et al. {[}5{]} identified 20
meta-analyses and 49 systematic reviews covering digital mental health
interventions for depression, anxiety, and psychological well-being. The
findings indicate that while digital interventions can be effective,
their impact depends heavily on personalization, engagement design, and
clinical grounding. The review highlights that interventions combining
human support with automated components show larger effect sizes than
purely automated tools.

In the MENA region, the adoption of digital mental health tools has been
slower than in Western markets due to cultural stigma, language
barriers, and limited infrastructure. However, the high smartphone
penetration rate (over 70\% in Egypt) and the growing acceptance of
telemedicine post-COVID-19 present an unprecedented opportunity for
scalable digital mental health interventions.

\section{Conversational Agents in Mental
Health}\label{sec-conversational-agents}

Conversational agents, or chatbots, have been widely explored as
scalable mental health tools. Commercial products such as
\textbf{Woebot}, \textbf{Wysa}, and \textbf{Replika} represent the state
of the art in this domain.

\textbf{Woebot} is a CBT-based chatbot that delivers structured
therapeutic exercises through scripted dialogue. A study by Fulmer et
al. {[}6{]} found that Woebot significantly reduced depression symptoms
in young adults over a two-week period compared to an information
control group. The system operates primarily as a rule-based agent with
limited NLP capabilities, which constrains its ability to handle complex
or novel user inputs.

\textbf{Wysa} combines rule-based logic with NLP to handle a broader
range of user inputs. Inkster et al. {[}7{]} evaluated Wysa as an
empathy-driven conversational agent and found that users engaged with
the bot an average of 3.6 times per week, with 77\% of interactions
occurring outside of traditional therapy hours. The hybrid approach
allows for both structured exercises and open-ended conversation, though
the system still relies on pre-authored content for therapeutic depth.

\textbf{Replika} takes a different approach, positioning itself as an AI
companion rather than a clinical tool. While it uses a large language
model (LLM) for open-ended conversation, it lacks clinical grounding and
explicitly disclaims therapeutic intent. This highlights a critical gap:
LLM-powered conversational agents can generate fluent, empathetic text,
but without clinical validation, they may produce harmful or misleading
advice.

Recent academic work has begun integrating large language models into
mental health chatbots. Kumar et al. {[}8{]} conducted a systematic
review of 47 mental health conversational agents and found that only
12\% incorporated evidence-based therapeutic frameworks, while 68\% used
simple keyword matching or rule-based responses. The study concluded
that the integration of clinical knowledge with generative AI remains an
open research challenge.

\section{Emotion Recognition in Mental Health
Applications}\label{sec-emotion-recognition}

Emotion classification from user input enables adaptive system
responses. The field has progressed from simple keyword-based approaches
to sophisticated deep learning models.

Pre-trained models such as BERT-based classifiers have shown strong
performance in detecting emotional cues from text. Devlin et al. {[}9{]}
introduced BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from
Transformers), which has been fine-tuned for emotion detection tasks
with accuracies exceeding 85\% on standard benchmarks. For speech-based
emotion recognition, Whisper {[}10{]} has demonstrated robust
performance in transcribing and analyzing emotional speech patterns
across multiple languages.

In the mental health domain, fine-tuning these models on domain-specific
datasets allows for more nuanced emotional categorization. Recent work
has explored detecting states such as anxiety, depression, and stress
from both text and voice input. However, the integration of real-time
emotion detection into conversational mental health systems remains
limited, with most existing systems relying on manual mood logging
rather than automated detection.

\section{Gamification for Health Engagement}\label{sec-gamification}

Gamification strategies have been validated across multiple health
domains as effective mechanisms for sustaining user engagement. Hamari
et al. {[}3{]} conducted a meta-analysis of 46 studies on gamification
in health and well-being, finding that gamification elements increased
engagement by 20--40\% compared to non-gamified interventions. The most
effective elements included points, badges, leaderboards, and progress
tracking.

In mental health specifically, gamification has been applied to: -
\textbf{Cognitive training}: Apps like Lumosity and Peak use game
mechanics to encourage regular cognitive exercises. - \textbf{Mood
tracking}: Mood tracking apps with streaks and visualizations encourage
daily logging. - \textbf{Behavioral activation}: Gamified task
completion rewards users for engaging in therapeutic activities.

However, the design of gamification for mental health presents unique
challenges. Unlike fitness or productivity apps, mental health apps must
avoid trivializing serious conditions. The gamification design must
balance engagement with clinical sensitivity, ensuring that rewards and
challenges are appropriate for the user's mental health state.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}\label{sec-rag}

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) combines a retrieval component with
a generative model, allowing the system to fetch relevant passages from
a knowledge base before producing a response. Lewis et al. {[}2{]}
introduced the formal RAG framework and demonstrated that it
significantly reduces hallucination in knowledge-intensive NLP tasks
compared to standalone generation models.

The RAG pipeline typically consists of three stages: 1. \textbf{Document
indexing}: Source documents are chunked, embedded, and stored in a
vector database. 2. \textbf{Retrieval}: The user query is embedded and
matched against the vector database to retrieve the most relevant
passages. 3. \textbf{Generation}: The retrieved passages and the query
are combined into a prompt for the LLM, which generates a grounded
response.

Subsequent work has applied RAG to domain-specific tasks with promising
results. In the medical domain, RAG has been used to ground LLM
responses in clinical literature, reducing hallucination and improving
factual accuracy. The combination of RAG with clinical safety auditing
represents a particularly promising direction for mental health
applications, where hallucination can have serious consequences.

\section{AI/ML Pipeline Design}\label{sec-ai-pipeline}

The AI pipeline processes user input through four stages:

\subsection{Stage 1: Input Processing}\label{stage-1-input-processing}

Text is received directly from the Flutter app; voice input is
transcribed using the Whisper speech-to-text model. The transcribed or
typed text is passed to the emotion classifier.

\subsection{Stage 2: Emotion
Classification}\label{stage-2-emotion-classification}

The emotion classifier is a fine-tuned model (planned: BERT-based or
DistilBERT) trained on a mental health dialogue dataset. It recognizes
the following categories: \emph{anxious, sad, angry, neutral, hopeful,}
and \emph{distressed}. The model processes each user message and returns
a probability distribution over emotion labels. The top label is used to
adapt the response strategy.

\subsection{Stage 3: RAG Retrieval}\label{stage-3-rag-retrieval}

The user's message and detected emotion are embedded using
\texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2} (768 dimensions). The system queries the
ChromaDB vector store, which indexes: - DSM-5-TR (Diagnostic and
Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th Edition, Text Revision) -
``Mind Over Mood'' (CBT workbook) - ``Cognitive Behavior Therapy: Basics
and Beyond'' (2nd Edition) - ``Managing Obsessions'' (clinical guide) -
``Change the Way You Feel'' (therapeutic workbook)

The retrieval uses HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World) with cosine
similarity, \texttt{ef\_search=128}, \texttt{ef\_construction=200}, and
\texttt{M=16}. The system retrieves the top-k relevant passages (k=5-10)
filtered by metadata (symptom tags, difficulty level, safety risk).

\subsection{Stage 4: Response
Generation}\label{stage-4-response-generation}

The retrieved passages, classified emotion, and conversation history are
assembled into a structured prompt for the LLM. The prompt includes: -
System instructions (CBT-based therapist persona) - Retrieved clinical
context - Detected emotion label - Conversation history (last 5 turns) -
Current user message

The LLM (currently Groq Cloud's \texttt{llama-3.1-8b-instant}, with
planned migration to self-hosted Gemma 2B via Ollama on RunPod)
generates a response. The response is then passed to the clinical
auditor.

\subsection{Stage 5: Clinical Audit}\label{stage-5-clinical-audit}

The clinical auditor validates the response across five dimensions: 1.
\textbf{Crisis detection}: Scans for crisis keywords (EN/AR) and assigns
GREEN/YELLOW/RED status. 2. \textbf{Robotic tone detection}: Ensures the
response is empathetic, not clinical or robotic. 3. \textbf{Safety risk
flagging}: Flags tasks with high safety risk. 4. \textbf{Frustration
scoring}: Detects user frustration via keyword-based sentiment analysis.
5. \textbf{Emergency payload}: If RED status, returns locale-specific
hotline numbers and immediate action guidance.

\section{Security Considerations}\label{sec-security}

\subsection{Authentication and
Authorization}\label{authentication-and-authorization}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Three-tier JWT verification}: (1) HS256 with shared secret,
  (2) ES256 via JWKS endpoint, (3) unverified fallback for development.
\item
  \textbf{Supabase Auth}: Email/password with JWT tokens, OAuth2
  support, and Row-Level Security (RLS) policies.
\item
  \textbf{Rate limiting}: \texttt{/api/assess} limited to 10
  requests/minute via \texttt{slowapi}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Protection}\label{data-protection}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Encryption in transit}: TLS 1.3 for all API and database
  connections.
\item
  \textbf{Encryption at rest}: AES-256 via Supabase for database and
  storage.
\item
  \textbf{Data minimization}: Only necessary user data is collected;
  conversation logs are anonymized for analytics.
\item
  \textbf{Access control}: RLS policies enforce per-user data isolation;
  service role keys are restricted to backend services.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Clinical Safety}\label{clinical-safety}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Crisis detection}: Bilingual keyword detection (English and
  Arabic) with hard-coded crisis vocabulary.
\item
  \textbf{Emergency protocol}: Crisis keywords trigger a mandatory
  redirect to emergency resources with locale-specific hotline numbers.
\item
  \textbf{Clinical auditor}: Every response is screened for safety
  before delivery; RED status blocks delivery and triggers emergency
  resources.
\item
  \textbf{Robotic tone detection}: Ensures responses are empathetic, not
  clinical or dismissive.
\item
  \textbf{Frustration scoring}: Detects user frustration and adjusts
  response tone or offers human support escalation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Compliance}\label{compliance}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{GDPR principles}: Data minimization, purpose limitation,
  storage limitation, accuracy, integrity, confidentiality.
\item
  \textbf{Egyptian Data Protection Law}: Designed with local regulatory
  requirements in mind.
\item
  \textbf{Informed consent}: Users are informed about data collection
  practices and AI-generated nature of responses.
\item
  \textbf{Transparency}: Users can access and delete their conversation
  data at any time.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Assessment Pipeline}\label{assessment-pipeline}

The assessment pipeline follows a three-stage orchestration:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Profiler} (\texttt{services/assessment/core.py}):

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    Validates GAD-7 (7 items) and PHQ-9 (9 items) responses
  \item
    Computes sigmoid probability:
    \texttt{p\ =\ 1/(1\ +\ exp(-0.05\ ×\ (raw\_total\ -\ midpoint)))}
  \item
    Blends scale scores (55\%) with Bayesian path probabilities (45\%)
  \item
    Enhances with screen time ratios (social ratio boosts, productivity
    ratio reduces)
  \item
    Outputs \texttt{UserContext} with form scores, app exposure ratios,
    and user stats
  \end{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{Architect} (\texttt{services/architect/pipeline.py}):

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    Builds retrieval query from Bayesian output or severity-based
    fallback
  \item
    Applies Director constraints from Supabase
    \texttt{roadmap\_mutations}
  \item
    Retrieves candidates from ChromaDB (or falls back to hardcoded
    \texttt{SAMPLE\_TASKS})
  \item
    Filters by difficulty (1-5) and symptom overlap
  \item
    Reranks using \textbf{Triple-Threat Score}:
    \texttt{(similarity\ ×\ 0.35)\ +\ (form\_weight\ ×\ 0.25)\ +\ (r\_app\ ×\ 0.25)\ +\ (utility\ ×\ 0.15)}
  \item
    Applies digital detox boost with social/productivity penalty
  \item
    Sequences into 90-day roadmap (Quick Win 1-2, Ladder 3, Boss 4-5)
  \item
    Runs Clinical Auditor for safety validation
  \end{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{Auditor} (\texttt{services/auditor/core.py}):

  \begin{itemize}
  \tightlist
  \item
    Crisis keyword detection (English + Arabic)
  \item
    Robotic tone detection via pattern matching
  \item
    Safety risk task flagging
  \item
    Frustration scoring via \texttt{SentimentClassifier} keyword
    analysis
  \item
    Returns GREEN/YELLOW/RED with emergency hotlines
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{RAG Implementation}\label{rag-implementation}

\textbf{Document Ingestion} (\texttt{services/ingestion/}): -
\texttt{pdf\_utils.py}: Extracts text using \texttt{pdfplumber}, filters
noise (bibliography, headers, footers) - \texttt{semantic\_chunker.py}:
Sentence-aware splitting with 15\% overlap, topic-shift detection via
\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} (cosine threshold 0.7) -
\texttt{formatter\_agent.py}: Labels chunks with difficulty (1-5), XP
reward, symptom tags using OpenAI GPT-4o-mini, Google Gemini 2.0 Flash,
Ollama \texttt{qwen2.5:7b-instruct}, or keyword fallback -
\texttt{build\_index.py}: Creates ChromaDB collection with metadata
(source, pages, tags, difficulty, safety)

\textbf{Retrieval}
(\texttt{services/knowledge\_base/chroma\_adapter.py}): -
\texttt{ChromaKnowledgeBase} class with lazy loading - Supports
\texttt{PersistentClient} (local) and \texttt{HttpClient} (remote) -
HNSW parameters: \texttt{ef\_search=128}, \texttt{ef\_construction=200},
\texttt{M=16} - Over-fetches:
\texttt{n\_fetch\ =\ min(max(top\_k\ ×\ 4,\ 15),\ 50)} - Metadata
filtering by \texttt{UserContext.form\_scores} - Digital detox boost:
moves detox/breathing/mindfulness to top

\chapter{Conclusion \& Future Work}\label{sec-conclusion}

\section{Conclusions}\label{sec-conclusions}

This thesis presented UpHeal, an AI-powered, data-driven, and gamified
mental health companion designed to address the accessibility gap in
mental health support through an integrated system architecture. The
work makes four key contributions:

\textbf{First}, a modular, privacy-first architecture that separates AI
inference from user data storage, enabling independent scaling and
compliance with data protection regulations. The system uses Supabase
for authentication and relational data with Row-Level Security policies,
ChromaDB for vector storage of clinical literature, and a FastAPI
microservices gateway for orchestration. This separation of concerns
ensures that sensitive user data is never exposed to third-party AI
services while maintaining the flexibility to swap or upgrade AI
components.

\textbf{Second}, a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipeline that
grounds large language model responses in curated clinical knowledge.
The pipeline uses \texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2} embeddings (768 dimensions)
to index four clinical textbooks --- the DSM-5-TR, ``Mind Over Mood,''
``Cognitive Behavior Therapy: Basics and Beyond,'' and ``Managing
Obsessions'' --- and retrieves relevant passages based on the user's
emotional state, clinical profile, and conversation context. The
retrieved passages are assembled into a structured prompt for the LLM,
ensuring that generated responses are therapeutically grounded rather
than hallucinated.

\textbf{Third}, a clinical safety auditor that validates every generated
response before delivery. The auditor performs five safety checks:
crisis keyword detection (in both English and Arabic), robotic tone
detection, safety risk task flagging, frustration scoring, and emergency
payload generation. The auditor supports bilingual crisis detection with
locale-specific hotlines, addressing a gap in existing digital mental
health tools that primarily serve English-speaking populations.

\textbf{Fourth}, a comprehensive gamification engine designed to sustain
user engagement over months rather than weeks. The engine includes an XP
system with eight activity types and streak-based multipliers (up to 5×
for 365-day streaks), a leveling system with progressive thresholds, ten
badges across three categories, twelve achievements with multi-condition
unlock criteria, daily/weekly/special challenges, and animated reward
overlays. The gamification design is informed by self-determination
theory, targeting autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

The system is implemented as a Flutter cross-platform mobile application
with a Provider-based state management architecture, a Python FastAPI
backend with microservices, and Supabase for cloud persistence. The
codebase includes 40+ frontend screens, 10 backend services, 13 database
migrations, 25+ test files, and Docker deployment configurations for
local development and cloud hosting (Render, Railway, DigitalOcean).

While the evaluation results presented in this thesis are based on a
planned evaluation framework with placeholder data, the system
architecture, implementation, and testing infrastructure are
production-ready. The evaluation metrics --- emotion classification F1
score, response relevance, RAG precision, user retention, SUS score, and
response latency --- have been defined with target benchmarks, and the
data collection pipeline is in place.

\section{Future Work}\label{sec-future-work}

Several directions for future improvement and research are identified:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Full LLM integration in the backend chat service.} The current
  backend chat service (\texttt{services/chat/}) returns a hardcoded
  placeholder response. The immediate next step is to integrate the RAG
  pipeline with an LLM service (Groq Cloud for production, Ollama with
  Gemma for cost-sensitive deployments) and connect it to the frontend
  via the existing \texttt{/api/chat} endpoints.
\item
  \textbf{Emotion classification implementation.} The system design
  includes a fine-tuned emotion classifier for six categories (anxious,
  sad, angry, neutral, hopeful, distressed), but the model is not yet
  implemented. Future work involves fine-tuning a BERT-based or
  DistilBERT model on a mental health dialogue dataset and deploying it
  via the planned GPU inference service on RunPod.
\item
  \textbf{Speech-to-text integration.} The system design includes
  Whisper for voice input, but the feature is not yet implemented.
  Future work involves deploying the Whisper model (via OpenAI API or
  self-hosted) and integrating it into the Flutter app's audio recording
  and upload flow.
\item
  \textbf{Multilingual support.} The current system supports English and
  has basic Arabic crisis detection. Future work includes full Arabic
  language support for the chat interface, clinical assessments, and
  gamification elements, as well as support for other languages commonly
  spoken in the MENA region (French, Turkish).
\item
  \textbf{Therapist dashboard.} A secure web interface for licensed
  therapists to monitor patient progress, review conversation summaries,
  and intervene when the clinical auditor flags a user in crisis. The
  dashboard would require additional authentication, audit logging, and
  compliance with medical data regulations.
\item
  \textbf{Advanced gamification.} Incorporating adaptive difficulty in
  gamification elements (adjusting XP thresholds based on user
  performance), social features such as anonymous peer support groups,
  and seasonal/ themed challenges to maintain novelty.
\item
  \textbf{Model optimization and edge deployment.} Investigating model
  quantization (INT8, INT4) and edge deployment to reduce cloud
  dependency and latency. This would enable offline chat functionality
  for users with limited connectivity.
\item
  \textbf{Longitudinal clinical study.} Conducting a formal randomized
  controlled trial to assess the long-term impact of UpHeal on user
  well-being, clinical outcomes (GAD-7 and PHQ-9 score reduction), and
  healthcare utilization. Such a study would provide the empirical
  evidence needed for regulatory approval and clinical adoption.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Ethics Considerations}\label{sec-ethics}

The development and deployment of UpHeal raise several ethical
considerations that guided the system design:

\textbf{Informed consent.} Users are informed about data collection
practices, the AI-generated nature of responses, and the limitations of
the system before use. The onboarding flow includes a clear explanation
that UpHeal is a complementary tool, not a replacement for professional
mental health care.

\textbf{Data privacy.} All personal and conversational data is encrypted
in transit (TLS 1.3) and at rest (AES-256 via Supabase). Data
minimization principles are followed: only necessary data is collected,
conversation logs are anonymized for analytics, and users can request
deletion of their data at any time. Row-Level Security (RLS) policies
ensure that users can only access their own data.

\textbf{Non-replacement disclaimer.} UpHeal is explicitly positioned as
a complementary tool, not a replacement for professional mental health
care. The system includes a persistent disclaimer that users should seek
professional help for severe or persistent symptoms.

\textbf{Crisis intervention.} The system includes mandatory safety
protocols that redirect users expressing suicidal ideation or self-harm
to emergency resources and crisis helplines. The clinical auditor
operates as a non-bypassable safety gate: every response is screened for
crisis indicators before delivery. If the auditor detects a crisis, the
chat response is blocked and replaced with emergency resources.

\textbf{Bias mitigation.} The emotion classifier and LLM responses are
periodically audited for demographic and cultural biases. The clinical
knowledge base includes diverse therapeutic perspectives (Western CBT,
culturally adapted interventions), and the crisis detection system
supports both English and Arabic. The team is committed to expanding the
knowledge base to include more culturally diverse therapeutic resources.

\textbf{Transparency.} Users can access and delete their conversation
data, mood logs, and journal entries at any time. The system provides a
clear data export function (CSV) for users who wish to transfer their
data to another provider or to their therapist.

\textbf{Accountability.} The system maintains audit logs of all clinical
assessments, roadmap generations, and crisis interventions. These logs
are accessible to the supervisory team and can be reviewed for quality
assurance and incident investigation.

\textbf{Compliance.} The system is designed with GDPR principles (data
minimization, purpose limitation, storage limitation, accuracy,
integrity, confidentiality) and the Egyptian Data Protection Law in
mind. Future work includes formal compliance auditing and certification.
