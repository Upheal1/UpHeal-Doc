\chapter*{Abstract}\label{abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

\markboth{Abstract}{Abstract}

The project introduces UpHeal, an AI-powered, data-driven, and gamified
mental health companion designed to provide accessible, empathetic, and
evidence-based mental health support through a mobile application. By
integrating large language models with emotion classification and
retrieval-augmented generation, the system delivers contextually
relevant therapeutic responses while maintaining user engagement through
gamification elements. The application leverages a Flutter-based
cross-platform frontend, a Python FastAPI backend, Supabase for
authentication and data management, and GPU-powered AI inference on
RunPod.

The aim is to practice applying the full spectrum of software
engineering, artificial intelligence, and user experience design skills
acquired throughout the undergraduate program. This includes
requirements engineering, system architecture, machine learning pipeline
design, backend and frontend development, testing, and evaluation within
a real-world healthcare domain that demands ethical responsibility, data
privacy, and clinical sensitivity.

The thesis is organized as follows:

Chapter 1 gives an introduction about the problem. Then moves to the
necessary background. The chapter moves to the problem definition and
proposed solution in brief.

Chapter 2 represents the literature review with recent publications
related to the project topic. From those, the existing solution comes as
an emerging technology to fill in the gaps. A detailed gap analysis is
presented as well.

Chapter 3 represents the most comprehensive chapter. This moves to the
system design and diagrams (Sequence Diagram, Use Case Diagram,
Functional Flow). The model architecture is then described with
necessary hardware considerations. Finally, security considerations are
explained.

Chapter 4 presents the system implementation and the obtained results.
All curves, charts, and comparisons are shown.

Chapter 5 gives the conclusions and future work. Finally, the adopted
ethics considerations are explained in brief.

\section{Background and Motivation}\label{sec-background}

Mental health disorders represent one of the leading causes of
disability worldwide. The World Health Organization estimates that one
in every eight people globally lives with a mental health condition, yet
the median coverage of mental health services in low- and middle-income
countries remains below 25\% {[}1{]}. In Egypt and the broader MENA
region, cultural stigma, a shortage of trained professionals, and
financial barriers compound the treatment gap, leaving millions without
adequate support.

The rapid proliferation of smartphones --- global penetration exceeded
6.8 billion devices in 2023 --- combined with advances in artificial
intelligence creates a unique opportunity to bridge this gap. Digital
mental health interventions can provide immediate, stigma-free, and
scalable assistance that complements traditional face-to-face therapy.
Over the past decade, the field has evolved from simple self-help
journals and static psychoeducation modules to sophisticated platforms
that leverage natural language processing, emotion recognition, and
behavioral gamification to deliver personalized support at scale.

Recent developments in large language models (LLMs) have further
transformed the landscape. Models such as LLaMA, Gemma, and GPT-4 have
demonstrated the ability to generate contextually relevant, empathetic
text that can be grounded in curated knowledge bases through
retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) {[}2{]}. When combined with
evidence-based therapeutic frameworks --- Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
(CBT), Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), and structured clinical
assessments such as the Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale
(GAD-7) and the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) --- these models
can produce responses that are not merely conversational but clinically
informed.

Simultaneously, gamification --- the application of game-design elements
to non-game contexts --- has proven effective at sustaining user
engagement in health applications. Meta-analyses indicate that
well-designed gamification elements, including points, badges, streaks,
and adaptive difficulty, can increase retention rates by 20--40\%
compared to ungameified counterparts {[}3{]}.

Despite these advances, a significant gap remains between the promise of
AI-assisted mental health tools and their practical deployment. Existing
solutions tend to address only one or two dimensions of the problem ---
either providing conversational support without clinical grounding, or
offering structured programs without personalization, or engaging users
initially without sustaining long-term adherence. A holistic solution
that integrates all of these dimensions within a privacy-first,
culturally sensitive framework does not yet exist at scale.

\section{Problem Definition}\label{sec-problem-definition}

Despite the growing number of digital mental health applications, four
fundamental challenges persist:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Lack of personalization}. Many chatbot-based tools provide
  generic, template-driven responses that fail to adapt to a user's
  current emotional state, clinical profile, or behavioral patterns. A
  person reporting mild anxiety on Monday and severe anxiety on Thursday
  may receive identical therapeutic suggestions, undermining both trust
  and clinical efficacy.
\item
  \textbf{Low user retention}. Without engaging design, users tend to
  abandon mental health applications within two to four weeks. A
  systematic review of mobile health app usage found that 77\% of users
  who download a health app discontinue use within the first three days,
  and fewer than 5\% remain active after 90 days {[}3{]}, {[}4{]}. The
  absence of progressive reward systems, social accountability, and
  adaptive difficulty curves contributes to this attrition.
\item
  \textbf{Limited clinical grounding}. Few applications integrate
  evidence-based therapeutic frameworks with AI-generated responses.
  Most commercial mental health chatbots operate as closed-domain
  retrieval systems, returning pre-authored scripts from a fixed
  response bank. Those that employ LLMs often do so without grounding
  the model's output in verified clinical literature, increasing the
  risk of hallucination --- generating plausible-sounding but factually
  incorrect or even harmful advice.
\item
  \textbf{Inadequate privacy and safety mechanisms}. Handling sensitive
  mental health data requires robust encryption, access controls, and
  ethical practices. Many commercial applications lack transparent data
  governance, do not implement crisis detection protocols, and fail to
  provide emergency resource redirection when users express suicidal
  ideation or self-harm intent. The absence of culturally localized
  crisis resources further limits the utility of these tools in
  non-Western markets.
\end{enumerate}

These challenges are not independent. Low personalization reduces
perceived value, which accelerates attrition. Attrition starves the
system of the longitudinal data needed to personalize effectively. And
without clinical grounding, even personalized responses can be
dangerous. Addressing these challenges requires an integrated approach.

\section{Proposed Solution}\label{sec-proposed-solution}

UpHeal is an AI-powered, data-driven, and gamified mental health
companion designed to address all four challenges simultaneously through
an integrated system architecture. The system combines four
complementary pillars:

\textbf{Pillar 1: RAG-augmented conversational AI.} UpHeal's chat
service does not rely on a standalone language model generating
responses in isolation. Instead, each user message triggers a
multi-stage pipeline: (a) an emotion classifier detects the user's
affective state from the input text, (b) a retrieval-augmented
generation pipeline queries a vector database of curated clinical
literature --- including the DSM-5-TR, CBT workbooks, and evidence-based
therapeutic protocols --- to identify passages relevant to the user's
emotional context and clinical profile, (c) the retrieved passages,
conversation history, and detected emotion label are assembled into a
structured prompt for a large language model (LLM), and (d) a clinical
auditor validates the generated response for safety, crisis indicators,
and therapeutic tone before delivery. This architecture ensures that
responses are both contextually personalized and clinically grounded.

\textbf{Pillar 2: Real-time emotion-adaptive interaction.} The system
continuously adapts its behavior based on the user's detected emotional
state. When a user's messages indicate elevated anxiety or sadness, the
system adjusts task difficulty, modifies response tone, and prioritizes
calming interventions such as guided breathing exercises. When the
system detects crisis keywords --- in both English and Arabic --- it
triggers a mandatory redirection to emergency resources with localized
hotline numbers. Emotion classification is powered by a fine-tuned model
that processes text input, with future support for voice-based detection
via Whisper speech-to-text transcription.

\textbf{Pillar 3: Comprehensive gamification engine.} UpHeal implements
a multi-layered gamification system designed to sustain engagement over
weeks and months rather than days. The engine includes: an experience
point (XP) system with eight activity types and streak-based multipliers
(up to 5× for 365-day streaks); a leveling system with progressive
thresholds; ten badges across streak, task completion, and
addiction-free categories; twelve achievements with multi-condition
unlock criteria; daily, weekly, and special challenges with time-based
expiration; and animated reward overlays that provide immediate positive
reinforcement. The gamification design draws on self-determination
theory, targeting autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

\textbf{Pillar 4: Privacy-first, safety-augmented architecture.} UpHeal
enforces encryption in transit (TLS 1.3) and at rest (AES-256 via
Supabase), row-level security (RLS) policies that isolate user data, and
a three-tier JWT authentication system (HS256, ES256/JWKS, development
fallback). The clinical auditor operates as a mandatory safety gate:
every generated response is screened for crisis keywords, robotic tone,
safety risks, and frustration indicators before delivery. The auditor
supports bilingual crisis detection (English and Arabic) and provides
locale-specific emergency hotlines.

The system architecture comprises a Flutter cross-platform mobile
application serving as the primary user interface, a Python FastAPI
backend orchestrating the AI pipeline through a microservices gateway,
Supabase providing authentication, PostgreSQL database persistence,
real-time subscriptions, and object storage, a ChromaDB vector store
indexed with sentence-transformer embeddings
(\texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2}, 768 dimensions) housing the clinical
knowledge base, and GPU-hosted inference services for the LLM, emotion
classifier, and speech-to-text components.

\chapter{Literature Review}\label{sec-literature-review}

\section{Overview of Digital Mental Health
Solutions}\label{sec-digital-mh-overview}

The landscape of digital mental health has evolved significantly over
the past decade, moving from simple self-help applications to
sophisticated AI-driven platforms. Early interventions relied primarily
on static content delivery, such as mood logs and psychoeducation
modules, while modern systems leverage natural language processing and
machine learning to offer dynamic, personalized support.

\section{Conversational Agents in Mental
Health}\label{sec-conversational-agents}

Conversational agents, or chatbots, have been widely explored as
scalable mental health tools. Woebot, Wysa, and Replika represent
notable commercial products that employ rule-based or hybrid approaches
to deliver therapeutic content.Woebot, for example, uses structured CBT
exercises through a scripted dialogue, while Wysa combines rule-based
logic with NLP to handle a broader range of user inputs.

Recent academic work has begun integrating large language models into
mental health chatbots. Studies such as those by {[}2{]} on
retrieval-augmented generation have demonstrated that grounding LLM
responses in curated knowledge bases can significantly reduce
hallucinations and improve factual accuracy---critical requirements in a
clinical setting.

\section{Emotion Recognition in Text and
Speech}\label{sec-emotion-recognition}

Emotion classification from user input enables adaptive system
responses. Pre-trained models such as BERT-based classifiers and Whisper
for speech-to-text transcription have shown strong performance in
detecting emotional cues {[}5{]}, {[}6{]}. Fine-tuning these models on
mental health-specific datasets allows for more nuanced emotional
categorization relevant to therapeutic contexts.

\section{Gamification for Health Engagement}\label{sec-gamification}

Gamification strategies including points, badges, streaks, and progress
tracking have been validated across multiple health domains as effective
mechanisms for sustaining user engagement. {[}3{]} provide a
meta-analysis showing that gamification elements, when thoughtfully
designed, increase retention rates in health applications by 20--40\%.

\section{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}\label{sec-rag}

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) combines a retrieval component with
a generative model, allowing the system to fetch relevant passages from
a knowledge base before producing a response. This approach mitigates
hallucination and ensures that generated content is grounded in verified
clinical sources. {[}2{]} introduced the formal framework, and
subsequent work has applied RAG to domain-specific tasks with promising
results.

\section{Functional Requirements}\label{sec-functional-requirements}

The following functional requirements define the core capabilities of
the UpHeal system:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{FR-01}: The system shall allow users to register and
  authenticate securely.
\item
  \textbf{FR-02}: The system shall provide an AI-powered conversational
  interface that responds to the user's mental health concerns.
\item
  \textbf{FR-03}: The system shall detect the user's emotional state
  from text and voice input.
\item
  \textbf{FR-04}: The system shall adapt its response style based on the
  detected emotion.
\item
  \textbf{FR-05}: The system shall allow users to log their mood on a
  daily basis.
\item
  \textbf{FR-06}: The system shall provide gamification elements
  including streaks, badges, and progress tracking.
\item
  \textbf{FR-07}: The system shall integrate a retrieval-augmented
  generation pipeline that grounds LLM responses in curated clinical
  knowledge.
\item
  \textbf{FR-08}: The system shall support speech-to-text input via the
  Whisper model.
\item
  \textbf{FR-09}: The system shall store conversation history securely
  for user review.
\item
  \textbf{FR-10}: The system shall provide an emergency resources page
  with crisis helpline information.
\end{itemize}

\section{AI/ML Pipeline Design}\label{sec-ai-pipeline}

The AI pipeline processes user input through three stages:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Input Processing}: Text is received directly; voice input is
  transcribed using Whisper.
\item
  \textbf{Emotion Classification}: The transcribed or typed text is
  passed through a fine-tuned emotion classifier to detect labels such
  as \emph{anxious}, \emph{sad}, \emph{angry}, \emph{neutral}, and
  \emph{hopeful}.
\item
  \textbf{Response Generation}: The classified emotion and user message
  are used to retrieve relevant clinical passages from the vector
  database. These passages, along with the conversation context, are fed
  into Gemma LLM via a structured prompt to generate a grounded,
  empathetic response.
\end{enumerate}

\section{AI Pipeline Implementation}\label{sec-ai-pipeline-impl}

\subsection{Emotion Classifier}\label{emotion-classifier}

The emotion classifier is fine-tuned on a mental health dialogue dataset
to recognize the following categories: \emph{anxious, sad, angry,
neutral, hopeful,} and \emph{distressed}. The model processes each user
message and returns a probability distribution over emotion labels, with
the top label used to adapt the response strategy.

\subsection{RAG Pipeline}\label{rag-pipeline}

The retrieval-augmented generation pipeline operates as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  The user message and detected emotion are embedded using the same
  vectorizer as the knowledge base.
\item
  The top-k relevant passages are retrieved from ChromaDB.
\item
  A structured prompt is constructed containing: system instructions,
  retrieved context, conversation history, and the current user message.
\item
  Gemma LLM generates a response conditioned on this prompt.
\item
  The response is post-processed to ensure safety and appended to the
  conversation.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Whisper Integration}\label{whisper-integration}

Voice messages are recorded in the Flutter app, uploaded to Supabase
Storage, and asynchronously transcribed using Whisper. The transcription
is then fed into the emotion classifier and RAG pipeline as described
above.

\chapter{Conclusion \& Future Work}\label{sec-conclusion}

\section{Conclusions}\label{sec-conclusions}

This thesis presented UpHeal, an AI-powered, data-driven, and gamified
mental health companion designed to address the accessibility gap in
mental health support. The system integrates large language models with
retrieval-augmented generation for clinically grounded responses,
real-time emotion classification for personalized interaction, and
gamification elements for sustained user engagement.

Key contributions of this work include:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  A modular, privacy-first architecture that separates AI inference from
  user data storage, enabling independent scaling and compliance with
  data protection regulations.
\item
  A RAG-augmented conversational pipeline that grounds LLM responses in
  curated clinical knowledge, reducing hallucinations and improving
  therapeutic relevance.
\item
  An emotion-adaptive interaction model that detects the user's
  emotional state and adjusts the response accordingly.
\item
  A gamification framework that promotes daily engagement through
  streaks, badges, and mood trend visualization.
\end{itemize}

The evaluation results demonstrate that UpHeal achieves {[}to be
completed after testing{]} in emotion classification accuracy, {[}X/5{]}
in response relevance as rated by human evaluators, and a System
Usability Scale score of {[}X/100{]}.

\section{Future Work}\label{sec-future-work}

Several directions for future improvement and research are identified:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Multilingual support}: Extending the system to support Arabic
  and other languages commonly spoken in Egypt and the MENA region.

  outcomes.

\section{Ethics Considerations}\label{sec-ethics}

The development and deployment of UpHeal raise several ethical
considerations:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Informed consent}: Users are informed about data collection
  practices and AI-generated nature of responses before use.
\item
  \textbf{Data privacy}: All personal and conversational data is
  encrypted and access-controlled. Data minimization principles are
  followed.
\item
  \textbf{Non-replacement disclaimer}: UpHeal is explicitly positioned
  as a complementary tool, not a replacement for professional mental
  health care.
\item
  \textbf{Crisis intervention}: The system includes mandatory safety
  protocols that redirect users expressing suicidal ideation or
  self-harm to emergency resources and crisis helplines.
\item
  \textbf{Bias mitigation}: Emotion classifiers and LLM responses are
  periodically audited for demographic and cultural biases.
\item
  \textbf{Transparency}: Users can access and delete their conversation
  data at any time, in compliance with data protection regulations.
\end{itemize}
