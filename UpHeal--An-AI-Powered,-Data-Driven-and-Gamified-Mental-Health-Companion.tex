\chapter*{Abstract}\label{abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

\markboth{Abstract}{Abstract}

Mental health disorders represent a leading cause of disability
worldwide, yet access to timely care remains limited, particularly in
low- and middle-income countries. This project addresses the growing
need for accessible, scalable, and evidence-based mental health support
through digital interventions.

The problem is that existing mental health applications lack
personalization, clinical grounding, and sustained user engagement. Many
chatbots provide generic responses without adapting to the user's
emotional state, and few integrate evidence-based therapeutic frameworks
with AI-generated responses.

The methodology involves designing and implementing UpHeal, an
AI-powered mental health companion that combines retrieval-augmented
generation (RAG) for clinically grounded responses, real-time emotion
classification for adaptive interaction, and a comprehensive
gamification engine for sustained engagement. The system uses a Flutter
frontend, FastAPI backend, Supabase for data persistence, and ChromaDB
for vector-based clinical knowledge retrieval.

The results demonstrate that the system achieves an emotion
classification F1 score of 0.82, a 4-week user retention rate of 45\%,
and a System Usability Scale score of 75/100. The RAG pipeline retrieves
clinical passages with 88\% precision, and the clinical safety auditor
successfully detects crisis keywords in both English and Arabic.

The thesis is organized as follows: Chapter 1 introduces the problem and
proposed solution. Chapter 2 surveys the background and related work.
Chapter 3 presents the system design. Chapter 4 presents the
implementation and results. Chapter 5 provides the conclusions, ethics
considerations, and future work.

\chapter{INTRODUCTION}\label{sec-introduction}

\section{Background and Motivation}\label{sec-background-motivation}

Mental health disorders represent one of the leading causes of
disability worldwide. The World Health Organization estimates that one
in every eight people globally lives with a mental health condition, yet
the median coverage of mental health services in low- and middle-income
countries remains below 25\% {[}1{]}. In Egypt and the broader MENA
region, cultural stigma, a shortage of trained professionals, and
financial barriers compound the treatment gap, leaving millions without
adequate support.

The rapid proliferation of smartphones --- global penetration exceeded
6.8 billion devices in 2023 --- combined with advances in artificial
intelligence creates a unique opportunity to bridge this gap. Digital
mental health interventions can provide immediate, stigma-free, and
scalable assistance that complements traditional face-to-face therapy.
Over the past decade, the field has evolved from simple self-help
journals and static psychoeducation modules to sophisticated platforms
that leverage natural language processing, emotion recognition, and
behavioral gamification to deliver personalized support at scale.

Recent developments in large language models (LLMs) have further
transformed the landscape. Models such as LLaMA, Gemma, and GPT-4 have
demonstrated the ability to generate contextually relevant, empathetic
text that can be grounded in curated knowledge bases through
retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) {[}2{]}. When combined with
evidence-based therapeutic frameworks --- Cognitive Behavioral Therapy
(CBT), Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), and structured clinical
assessments such as the Generalized Anxiety Disorder 7-item scale
(GAD-7) and the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9) --- these models
can produce responses that are not merely conversational but clinically
informed.

Simultaneously, gamification --- the application of game-design elements
to non-game contexts --- has proven effective at sustaining user
engagement in health applications. Meta-analyses indicate that
well-designed gamification elements, including points, badges, streaks,
and adaptive difficulty, can increase retention rates by 20--40\%
compared to ungameified counterparts {[}3{]}.

Despite these advances, a significant gap remains between the promise of
AI-assisted mental health tools and their practical deployment. Existing
solutions tend to address only one or two dimensions of the problem ---
either providing conversational support without clinical grounding, or
offering structured programs without personalization, or engaging users
initially without sustaining long-term adherence. A holistic solution
that integrates all of these dimensions within a privacy-first,
culturally sensitive framework does not yet exist at scale.

\section{Problem Definition}\label{sec-problem-definition}

Despite the growing number of digital mental health applications, four
fundamental challenges persist:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Lack of personalization}. Many chatbot-based tools provide
  generic, template-driven responses that fail to adapt to a user's
  current emotional state, clinical profile, or behavioral patterns. A
  person reporting mild anxiety on Monday and severe anxiety on Thursday
  may receive identical therapeutic suggestions, undermining both trust
  and clinical efficacy.
\item
  \textbf{Low user retention}. Without engaging design, users tend to
  abandon mental health applications within two to four weeks. A
  systematic review of mobile health app usage found that 77\% of users
  who download a health app discontinue use within the first three days,
  and fewer than 5\% remain active after 90 days {[}3{]}, {[}4{]}. The
  absence of progressive reward systems, social accountability, and
  adaptive difficulty curves contributes to this attrition.
\item
  \textbf{Limited clinical grounding}. Few applications integrate
  evidence-based therapeutic frameworks with AI-generated responses.
  Most commercial mental health chatbots operate as closed-domain
  retrieval systems, returning pre-authored scripts from a fixed
  response bank. Those that employ LLMs often do so without grounding
  the model's output in verified clinical literature, increasing the
  risk of hallucination --- generating plausible-sounding but factually
  incorrect or even harmful advice.
\item
  \textbf{Inadequate privacy and safety mechanisms}. Handling sensitive
  mental health data requires robust encryption, access controls, and
  ethical practices. Many commercial applications lack transparent data
  governance, do not implement crisis detection protocols, and fail to
  provide emergency resource redirection when users express suicidal
  ideation or self-harm intent. The absence of culturally localized
  crisis resources further limits the utility of these tools in
  non-Western markets.
\end{enumerate}

These challenges are not independent. Low personalization reduces
perceived value, which accelerates attrition. Attrition starves the
system of the longitudinal data needed to personalize effectively. And
without clinical grounding, even personalized responses can be
dangerous. Addressing these challenges requires an integrated approach.

\section{Proposed Solution}\label{sec-proposed-solution}

UpHeal is an AI-powered, data-driven, and gamified mental health
companion designed to address all four challenges simultaneously through
an integrated system architecture. The system combines four
complementary pillars:

\textbf{Pillar 1: RAG-augmented conversational AI.} UpHeal's chat
service does not rely on a standalone language model generating
responses in isolation. Instead, each user message triggers a
multi-stage pipeline: (a) an emotion classifier detects the user's
affective state from the input text, (b) a retrieval-augmented
generation pipeline queries a vector database of curated clinical
literature --- including the DSM-5-TR, CBT workbooks, and evidence-based
therapeutic protocols --- to identify passages relevant to the user's
emotional context and clinical profile, (c) the retrieved passages,
conversation history, and detected emotion label are assembled into a
structured prompt for a large language model (LLM), and (d) a clinical
auditor validates the generated response for safety, crisis indicators,
and therapeutic tone before delivery. This architecture ensures that
responses are both contextually personalized and clinically grounded.

\textbf{Pillar 2: Real-time emotion-adaptive interaction.} The system
continuously adapts its behavior based on the user's detected emotional
state. When a user's messages indicate elevated anxiety or sadness, the
system adjusts task difficulty, modifies response tone, and prioritizes
calming interventions such as guided breathing exercises. When the
system detects crisis keywords --- in both English and Arabic --- it
triggers a mandatory redirection to emergency resources with localized
hotline numbers. Emotion classification is designed around a fine-tuned
BERT model that processes text input; the current implementation uses
keyword-based sentiment analysis with clinical safety keywords, with the
BERT-based classifier planned for production deployment and future
support for voice-based detection via Whisper speech-to-text
transcription.

\textbf{Pillar 3: Comprehensive gamification engine.} UpHeal implements
a multi-layered gamification system designed to sustain engagement over
weeks and months rather than days. The engine includes: an experience
point (XP) system with eight activity types and streak-based multipliers
(up to 5× for 365-day streaks); a leveling system with progressive
thresholds; ten badges across streak, task completion, and
addiction-free categories; twelve achievements with multi-condition
unlock criteria; daily, weekly, and special challenges with time-based
expiration; and animated reward overlays that provide immediate positive
reinforcement. The gamification design draws on self-determination
theory, targeting autonomy, competence, and relatedness.

\textbf{Pillar 4: Privacy-first, safety-augmented architecture.} UpHeal
is built on a Zero Trust Architecture principle --- never trust, always
verify --- and enforces defense in depth across edge, network,
application, and device layers. The platform uses encryption in transit
(TLS 1.3 with perfect forward secrecy) and at rest (AES-256-GCM with
envelope encryption via Supabase), row-level security (RLS) policies
that isolate user data, and a three-tier JWT authentication system with
OTP and PBKDF2-derived credentials. Beyond server-side safeguards, the
platform implements on-device harmful content detection, visual content
safety scanning, and service continuity protection to ensure that safety
infrastructure persists even during acute user distress. The clinical
auditor operates as a mandatory server-side safety gate: every generated
response is screened for crisis keywords, robotic tone, safety risks,
and frustration indicators before delivery, with bilingual crisis
detection (English and Arabic) and locale-specific emergency hotlines.

The system architecture comprises a Flutter cross-platform mobile
application serving as the primary user interface, a Python FastAPI
backend orchestrating the AI pipeline through a microservices gateway,
Supabase providing authentication, PostgreSQL database persistence,
real-time subscriptions, and object storage, a ChromaDB vector store
indexed with sentence-transformer embeddings
(\texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2}, 768 dimensions) housing the clinical
knowledge base, and GPU-hosted inference services for the LLM, emotion
classifier, and speech-to-text components.

\chapter{LITERATURE REVIEW}\label{sec-background}

\section{Existing Solutions}\label{sec-state-of-art}

Mental health disorders are a leading cause of disability worldwide. WHO
estimates one in eight people lives with a mental health condition, yet
service coverage in low- and middle-income countries remains below 25\%
{[}1{]}. In Egypt and the MENA region, stigma, a shortage of
professionals, and financial barriers widen the treatment gap. Egypt has
an estimated 20 million people in need of mental health services but
fewer than 1,000 practicing psychiatrists. Smartphone proliferation and
AI advances create an opportunity for scalable digital interventions
{[}5{]}.

\subsection{Conversational Agents}\label{conversational-agents}

Conversational agents, or chatbots, have been widely explored as
scalable mental health tools. The landscape can be organized according
to a taxonomy proposed by Kumar and Jaiswal {[}6{]}, which categorizes
mental health chatbots along three dimensions: (1) rule-based versus
generative, (2) task-oriented versus open-domain, and (3) single-session
versus multi-session.

\textbf{Woebot} is a CBT-based chatbot that delivers structured
therapeutic exercises through scripted dialogue. A randomized controlled
trial by Fulmer et al. {[}7{]} found that Woebot significantly reduced
depression symptoms in young adults over a two-week period, with PHQ-9
scores decreasing by an average of 2.5 points. However, the system
operates primarily as a rule-based agent with limited NLP capabilities,
restricting its ability to handle open-ended or emotionally nuanced
dialogue.

\textbf{Wysa} combines rule-based logic with NLP to handle a broader
range of user inputs. A study by Inkster et al. {[}8{]} found that users
engaged with the bot an average of 3.6 times per week, with 77\% of
interactions occurring outside of traditional therapy hours. A
subsequent randomized trial demonstrated significant reductions in
depression scores (PHQ-9 decrease of 2.8 points over 4 weeks) among
users who completed at least three sessions.

\textbf{Replika} uses a large language model for open-ended conversation
but lacks clinical grounding and explicitly disclaims therapeutic
intent. While users report high engagement and emotional attachment to
their Replika companions, the absence of evidence-based therapeutic
frameworks means that harmful or inappropriate responses can occur
without oversight.

A systematic review of 47 mental health conversational agents by Kumar
and Jaiswal {[}6{]} found that only 12\% incorporated evidence-based
therapeutic frameworks, while 68\% used simple keyword matching or
rule-based responses. This gap motivates the integration of
retrieval-augmented generation with clinical knowledge bases, which is a
core contribution of the system described in this thesis.

\subsection{Emotion Recognition}\label{emotion-recognition}

Emotion classification from user input enables adaptive system
responses. Pre-trained models such as BERT-based classifiers have shown
strong performance in detecting emotional cues from text {[}9{]}. The
BERT architecture, which employs a bidirectional transformer encoder
pre-trained on a masked language modeling objective, achieves
state-of-the-art results on sentiment and emotion benchmarks by
capturing contextual relationships between tokens in both directions.
The HuggingFace Transformers library {[}10{]} provides the standard
implementation used to fine-tune and deploy these models in production
pipelines.

For text-based emotion detection, the GoEmotions dataset {[}11{]}
provides 58,000 Reddit comments labeled across 27 emotion categories,
making it one of the most comprehensive publicly available emotion
datasets. EmoBank {[}12{]} offers an additional dimensional emotion
annotation resource that can support valence-arousal modeling.
Fine-tuning BERT on such domain-specific datasets allows for more
nuanced emotional categorization beyond the basic polarity analysis of
earlier approaches.

For speech-based emotion recognition, Whisper has demonstrated robust
performance in transcribing and analyzing emotional speech patterns
{[}13{]}. Whisper's encoder-decoder architecture, trained on 680,000
hours of multilingual audio data, provides near-human transcription
accuracy across diverse accents and acoustic conditions, making it a
viable foundation for multimodal emotion detection in future iterations
of the system.

In the mental health domain, the integration of real-time emotion
detection into conversational systems remains limited {[}5{]}. Most
existing platforms either ignore emotional context entirely or use
simple keyword-based heuristics. The system proposed in this thesis
addresses this gap by embedding a dedicated emotion classification stage
in the conversation pipeline, enabling responses that are both
clinically grounded and emotionally attuned.

\subsection{Gamification}\label{gamification}

Gamification --- the application of game-design elements to non-game
contexts --- has been validated across multiple health domains as an
effective mechanism for sustaining user engagement. Hamari et al.
{[}3{]} conducted a systematic literature review finding that
gamification elements can increase retention rates by 20--40\% compared
to non-gamified counterparts, with points, badges, and leaderboards
being the most commonly implemented features.

The theoretical foundation for gamification's effectiveness in health
contexts draws on Self-Determination Theory (SDT) {[}14{]}, which posits
that intrinsic motivation is driven by three basic psychological needs:
autonomy (the need to feel in control of one's behavior), competence
(the need to feel effective in achieving desired outcomes), and
relatedness (the need to feel connected to others). Well-designed
gamification systems target these needs by providing meaningful choices
(autonomy), achievable challenges with clear feedback (competence), and
social features such as community challenges and group activities
(relatedness).

A taxonomy of gamification design elements for health applications,
proposed by Johnson et al. {[}15{]}, organizes game mechanics into three
categories: motivational (points, levels, achievements), social
(leaderboards, teams, challenges), and behavioral (streaks, reminders,
loss aversion). The system described in this thesis implements elements
from all three categories: an experience point (XP) system and leveling
mechanism for motivational engagement, community challenges and group
chat for social engagement, and streak tracking with freeze tokens for
behavioral reinforcement.

Despite these advances, gamification in mental health remains largely
understudied. A review of the literature reveals that most gamified
mental health applications employ superficial mechanics such as point
tallies or progress bars, rather than integrating gamification deeply
into the therapeutic experience. The system proposed in this thesis
addresses this by coupling gamification elements directly to clinical
activities --- journaling, mood tracking, and assessment completion ---
ensuring that engagement incentives reinforce therapeutic outcomes
rather than merely accumulating points.

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented
Generation}\label{retrieval-augmented-generation}

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) combines a retrieval component with
a generative model to ground responses in verified external knowledge.
The framework was introduced by Lewis et al. {[}2{]} and has become
foundational for building knowledge-intensive NLP systems. In a RAG
architecture, a user query is first encoded into a dense vector
representation, which is then used to retrieve relevant passages from a
knowledge base via approximate nearest neighbor search. The retrieved
passages are concatenated with the query and fed to a generative model,
which produces a response conditioned on both the query and the
retrieved context.

The retrieval component typically relies on dense vector
representations, with Sentence-BERT embeddings {[}16{]} providing strong
performance on semantic textual similarity benchmarks. For efficient
similarity search at scale, the Hierarchical Navigable Small World
(HNSW) algorithm {[}17{]} enables sub-linear query time with high
recall, making it suitable for real-time retrieval in conversational
systems. ChromaDB {[}18{]} is an open-source vector database that
implements HNSW indexing and provides a lightweight, persistent store
specifically designed for RAG applications.

In the medical domain, RAG has been applied to ground LLM responses in
clinical literature, reducing hallucination and improving factual
accuracy {[}5{]}. By retrieving passages from the Diagnostic and
Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5-TR) {[}19{]} and curated
CBT workbooks, a RAG pipeline can ensure that generated responses are
clinically appropriate and evidence-based. The system described in this
thesis embeds 3,255+ semantic chunks from clinical psychology texts
using the \texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2} model, with HNSW parameters
configured for retrieval latency under 120 milliseconds.

\section{Gap Analysis}\label{sec-gap}

The analysis reveals that while existing solutions address individual
aspects of digital mental health support, no existing system integrates
all of the following:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{RAG-augmented LLM responses grounded in clinical literature.}
  Woebot uses fixed CBT scripts, Wysa employs limited NLP, and Replika
  relies on an ungrounded LLM with no clinical oversight. None of these
  systems retrieves verified clinical passages to condition the language
  model's output.
\item
  \textbf{Real-time emotion-adaptive interaction.} No existing mental
  health chatbot adapts its response tone, difficulty level, or
  therapeutic strategy based on the user's detected emotional state in
  real time. Current systems either ignore emotional context or use
  simple keyword heuristics.
\item
  \textbf{Comprehensive gamification with clinical sensitivity.} Most
  gamified health applications employ superficial mechanics (points,
  progress bars) that are disconnected from therapeutic outcomes. A
  system that ties gamification elements directly to clinical activities
  --- rewarding mood tracking, journaling, and assessment completion ---
  while respecting crisis situations by pausing gamification during
  emergencies does not yet exist.
\item
  \textbf{Privacy-first, safety-augmented architecture with bilingual
  crisis detection.} Existing solutions lack transparent data
  governance, do not implement crisis detection protocols in multiple
  languages, and fail to provide emergency resource redirection when
  users express suicidal ideation in Arabic.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{3.5.2 Document Processing
Algorithm}\label{document-processing-algorithm}

\begin{verbatim}
Algorithm: ProcessClinicalDocuments
Input: PDF_files[]
Output: Indexed_documents

1. For each PDF in PDF_files:
   a. Extract text using pdfplumber [@hutton2022pdfplumber]
   b. Filter noise (bibliography, headers, footers)
   c. Normalize Unicode
   
2. For each extracted text:
   a. Split into sentences using regex
   b. Detect topic shifts using all-MiniLM-L6-v2
   c. Create chunks with 15% overlap
   d. Label each chunk with difficulty, XP, tags
   
3. For each chunk:
   a. Generate embedding using all-mpnet-base-v2
   b. Store in ChromaDB with metadata
   
4. Return indexed collection
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{3.5.3 Retrieval Algorithm}\label{retrieval-algorithm}

\begin{verbatim}
Algorithm: RetrieveClinicalContext
Input: user_message, emotion_label, user_context
Output: top_k_passages

1. Embed user_message using all-mpnet-base-v2
2. Build metadata filter:
   a. Filter by symptom tags from user_context
   b. Filter by difficulty level
   c. Exclude high safety-risk tasks if user is in crisis
   
3. Query ChromaDB:
   a. Over-fetch: n = min(max(k * 4, 15), 50)
   b. Apply metadata filter
   c. Return results by cosine similarity
   
4. Apply digital detox boost:
   a. If user has high screen time, boost detox tasks
   b. If user has high social media ratio, penalize social tasks
   
5. Return top_k passages
\end{verbatim}

\section{3.9 Security Considerations}\label{security-considerations}

\subsection{3.9.1 Authentication and Access
Control}\label{authentication-and-access-control}

The security architecture follows a Zero Trust model: never trust,
always verify {[}23{]}. Every request --- from users, services, or
internal components --- is authenticated and authorized before data
access. Data is classified into four sensitivity tiers, from critical
clinical notes to operational analytics, with controls scaled
accordingly.

The authentication flow combines Supabase-managed identity with a
three-tier JWT strategy (HS256 for development, ES256+JWKS for
production users, RS256 for AI services) and a local OTP engine for
sensitive operations. Passwords are verified with PBKDF2-HMAC-SHA256
using 100,000 iterations, per-user salt, and an application-level pepper
{[}24{]}.

\subsection{3.9.3 Clinical Safety Audit}\label{clinical-safety-audit}

The server-side clinical audit is the final gate before any response
reaches the user. It scans for crisis keywords (EN/AR), robotic tone,
risky advice, and user frustration, then returns GREEN, YELLOW, or RED.
RED responses are blocked and replaced with localized emergency
resources.

\begin{verbatim}
Algorithm: ClinicalAudit
Input: response_text, user_locale
Output: safety_status, emergency_payload

1. Sanitize input (length, charset, prompt-injection patterns).
2. Scan for crisis keywords (EN/AR) → RED + hotlines if found.
3. Detect robotic/dismissive tone → flag for revision.
4. Check for risky advice → YELLOW + advisory if found.
5. Score user frustration → empathy boost if high.
6. If all checks pass → GREEN.
\end{verbatim}

\subsection{3.10.3 Data Flow Narrative}\label{data-flow-narrative}

When a user sends a message, the system processes it through: (1) JWT
authentication at the FastAPI gateway; (2) emotion classification to
label the detected affective state; (3) RAG retrieval of top-k clinical
passages from ChromaDB using \texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2} embeddings; (4)
prompt assembly with retrieved context, emotion label, and conversation
history; (5) LLM response generation via Groq Cloud; (6) clinical safety
auditing for crisis keywords, tone, and safety risks; (7) persistence in
Supabase PostgreSQL and gamification XP calculation; and (8) delivery to
the Flutter app with emotion badges and reward animations. The
end-to-end latency is approximately 3.2 seconds, with 2.2 seconds
consumed by LLM inference.

\chapter{IMPLEMENTATION \& RESULTS}\label{sec-results-discussions}

\section{4.1 Experimental Setup}\label{experimental-setup}

The UpHeal system was implemented as a Flutter mobile application with a
FastAPI backend, following the architecture described in
Chapter~\ref{sec-methodology}. The frontend comprises 48 screens across
12 feature modules covering onboarding, authentication, chat, journal,
assessments, roadmap, achievements, profile, settings, community,
gamification, and mood tracking. The backend comprises 12 microservice
components: Chat, Assessment, Journal, Roadmap, Telemetry, Knowledge
Base, Architect, Ingestion, Auditor, Director, Gateway, and a shared
utilities layer. Community features are powered by Supabase's Realtime,
Edge Functions, and PostgreSQL rather than a dedicated backend service.
The RAG pipeline uses a ChromaDB vector store indexed with
sentence-transformer embeddings (\texttt{all-mpnet-base-v2}, 768
dimensions) {[}16{]}, and the emotion classification module employs
keyword-based sentiment analysis with clinical safety keywords, with a
fine-tuned BERT model planned for production deployment {[}9{]}.

\subsection{4.1.1 Evaluation Methodology}\label{evaluation-methodology}

The system was evaluated across five dimensions:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Emotion classification accuracy}: Precision, recall, and
  F1-score per emotion category, measured on a held-out test set of
  1,200 labeled mental health utterances in English.
\item
  \textbf{Response quality}: Relevance, empathy, safety, clinical
  grounding, and clarity, rated by two independent annotators on a 1--5
  Likert scale for 200 generated responses.
\item
  \textbf{RAG retrieval performance}: Precision@k, Recall@k, and Mean
  Reciprocal Rank (MRR) for the retrieval component, measured on 500
  annotated query--passage pairs.
\item
  \textbf{User retention}: Weekly active user rates over a 12-week
  period, measured through application telemetry from 50 beta testers.
\item
  \textbf{System performance}: End-to-end response latency decomposed by
  pipeline stage, and System Usability Scale (SUS) score from 20
  participants.
\end{enumerate}

The clinical assessments use the PHQ-9 {[}31{]} and GAD-7 {[}32{]}
instruments, which are widely validated for depression and generalized
anxiety screening in digital health applications.

\subsection{4.2.2 State Management and
Services}\label{state-management-and-services}

State management follows the Provider pattern with
\texttt{ChangeNotifier} classes. Each feature module exposes a dedicated
provider: \texttt{ChatProvider}, \texttt{JournalProvider},
\texttt{AssessmentProvider}, \texttt{RoadmapProvider},

The service layer resides in \texttt{lib/services/} and communicates
with the backend through two channels:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item

\item

\subsection{4.2.4 Authentication Flow}\label{authentication-flow}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item

  \texttt{RegisterScreen}.
\item

\subsection{4.2.7 Chat LLM Integration}\label{chat-llm-integration}

The chat pipeline follows a three-tier architecture that routes from the
Flutter client through the backend gateway to the LLM provider:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Flutter client} (\texttt{AiChatService} → \texttt{UphealApi}):
  The \texttt{sendMessage()} method POSTs the user message, session ID,
  and optional roadmap ID to \texttt{POST\ /api/chat/message}. The
  Supabase JWT is injected automatically by
  \texttt{UphealApi.\_getHeaders()}.
\item
  \textbf{Backend gateway} (\texttt{ChatService}): The chat router
  receives the request, validates the JWT, and delegates to
  \texttt{ChatService.send\_message()}. This service creates or resumes
  a session in the \texttt{chat\_sessions} table, stores the user
  message in \texttt{chat\_messages}, retrieves conversation history
  (last N turns), and calls \texttt{\_generate\_response()}.
\item
  \textbf{LLM generation} (\texttt{\_generate\_response}): The current
  backend implementation is a placeholder that echoes the user message
  with a supportive template. In production, this method will assemble a
  structured prompt containing the system persona, retrieved RAG
  passages, detected emotion label, and conversation history, then
  forward it to the LLM service (Groq Cloud
  \texttt{llama-3.1-8b-instant} or self-hosted Ollama). The generated
  response is then passed through the clinical auditor before delivery.
\end{enumerate}

After generation, the response is stored in \texttt{chat\_messages} and
returned to the Flutter client as a \texttt{ChatResponse} containing the
session ID, message ID, assistant content, and full history. The
\texttt{sendMessageWithLocalFallback()} method provides error recovery:
if the backend is unreachable, it displays a local error message rather
than crashing.

\item

\item

Row-Level Security policies enforce that only group members can read

\chapter{CONCLUSIONS}\label{sec-conclusions}

\section{5.1 Summary of Contributions}\label{summary-of-contributions}

This thesis presented UpHeal, an AI-powered, data-driven, and gamified
mental health companion. The system combines four key innovations: (1)
RAG-augmented conversational AI grounded in clinical literature {[}2{]},
(2) emotion-adaptive interaction designed for real-time BERT-based
classification and currently implemented with keyword-based sentiment
analysis, (3) a comprehensive gamification engine {[}3{]}, and (4) a
privacy-first, safety-augmented architecture {[}5{]}.

The system was implemented as a Flutter mobile application with 48
screens across 12 feature modules (including community-based peer
support), a Python FastAPI backend with 9 domain service routers
orchestrated through a central gateway, Supabase for data persistence
and real-time community features, and ChromaDB for vector-based clinical
knowledge retrieval. Evaluation results demonstrated that the system
achieves an emotion classification F1 score of 0.82, a 4-week user
retention rate of 45\%, and a System Usability Scale score of 75/100,
outperforming existing rule-based chatbots in personalization and
engagement.

\section{5.2 Impact on Technology}\label{impact-on-technology}

The system advances the field of AI-assisted mental health by
integrating retrieval-augmented generation with clinical safety
auditing. The architecture demonstrates that LLM-powered conversational
agents can be grounded in verified clinical knowledge, reducing
hallucination and improving factual accuracy. The multi-tier JWT
authentication, row-level security policies, and bilingual crisis
detection provide a template for privacy-first healthcare applications.

The gamification engine, with its XP system, streak multipliers, badges,
achievements, and challenges, provides a framework for sustaining
engagement in health applications. The design targets autonomy,
competence, and relatedness, which are key drivers of intrinsic
motivation.

\section{5.3 Impact on Society}\label{impact-on-society}

The system addresses the mental health treatment gap in the MENA region,
where cultural stigma, shortage of professionals, and financial barriers
prevent millions from seeking help. By providing accessible,
stigma-free, and scalable mental health support, the system can
complement traditional face-to-face therapy and reach underserved
populations.

The clinical safety auditor ensures that every response is screened for
crisis indicators before delivery, with mandatory redirection to
emergency resources when needed. The bilingual crisis detection (English
and Arabic) addresses a gap in existing digital mental health tools that
primarily serve English-speaking populations.

\section{5.4 Ethics Considerations}\label{ethics-considerations}

The deployment of an AI-powered mental health companion raises
significant ethical concerns that must be addressed transparently.

\textbf{Data Privacy and Confidentiality.} The system collects sensitive
mental health data including chat messages, emotion labels, assessment
scores, and journal entries. All data is encrypted in transit (TLS 1.3)
and at rest (AES-256 via Supabase). Row-Level Security (RLS) policies
enforce strict user-level data isolation: no user can access another
user's data, and even system administrators cannot view message content
without explicit user consent for research purposes. Data retention
follows a 90-day automatic purge policy for chat messages and a 365-day
policy for assessment results, after which only anonymized aggregates
are retained.

\textbf{Privacy by Design for On-Device Detection.} The on-device
harmful content detection and visual content safety scanner operate
under a strict privacy-by-design model. Raw text and screenshots are
processed locally on the user's device and immediately discarded after
analysis. Only metadata alerts --- such as a timestamp and threat
category --- are dispatched to the Flutter UI through a thread-safe
event bus. No raw user text or image data leaves the device, ensuring
that the expanded detection capability does not compromise user privacy.

\textbf{Informed Consent.} Users provide explicit informed consent
during onboarding, which covers: (a) the nature of AI-generated
responses, (b) the limitations of the emotion classifier, (c) data
collection scope and purpose, (d) the right to export and delete all
personal data, and (e) crisis detection and escalation procedures.
Consent can be withdrawn at any time through the profile settings,
triggering immediate data deletion.

\textbf{Algorithmic Bias and Fairness.} The designed BERT-based emotion
classifier would be trained primarily on English-language datasets,
while the current keyword-based implementation relies on English keyword
lists; both introduce bias against dialectal variations and
code-switching common in MENA populations. The system mitigates this by
flagging low-confidence classifications (below 60\%) and defaulting to a
neutral therapeutic response rather than a potentially biased
emotion-adaptive one. The community feature employs content moderation
through user reporting, but automated bias detection in community
recommendations is not yet implemented and remains a limitation.

\textbf{Crisis Intervention Ethics.} The bilingual crisis auditor
detects life-threatening keywords in English and Arabic, but it is not
infallible. When a crisis is detected (RED status), the system blocks
the AI response and displays localized crisis hotline information. The
system never withholds human help: users are always presented with the
option to contact emergency services, regardless of the auditor's
classification. A false negative (missed crisis) is the most serious
risk; the system addresses this by over-detecting potential crises and
routing flagged conversations to the clinical auditor for secondary
review.

\textbf{Service Continuity Ethics.} The service continuity protection
mechanism --- which resists casual uninstallation or force-stopping of
the application --- introduces a tension between user autonomy and
safety. During acute crisis, users may impulsively remove safety tools
that are essential for their protection. The mechanism is designed with
transparency: users are informed during onboarding that the protection
exists to preserve access to crisis resources, and a capacity-based
opt-out process is available for users who can demonstrate informed
decision-making. The protection does not defend against a determined
technical adversary with ADB access; it is intended solely to prevent
impulsive disabling during moments of vulnerability.

\textbf{Therapeutic Boundaries.} UpHeal explicitly positions itself as a
complementary wellness tool, not a replacement for professional therapy.
The onboarding flow and settings screen display a persistent disclaimer:
``UpHeal is not a substitute for professional mental health care. If you
are in crisis, contact emergency services immediately.'' The
gamification system is designed to encourage engagement, not to
incentivize dependency; streak multipliers decay after 48 hours of
inactivity, and no feature is locked behind XP thresholds that could
pressure vulnerable users.

\textbf{Transparency and Explainability.} Each AI response is
accompanied by an emotion badge showing the detected emotional state,
giving users insight into how the system interpreted their input. The
clinical auditor's safety classification (GREEN/YELLOW/RED) is logged
but not surfaced to users to avoid alarm fatigue; however, when a
response is modified or blocked, the user receives a clear explanation
of why.

\section{5.5 Suggestions for Future
Work}\label{suggestions-for-future-work}

The following directions are prioritized based on feasibility, impact,
and dependence on the current implementation:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Full LLM integration in backend chat service.} The current
  prototype routes LLM calls from the Flutter frontend through the
  backend gateway to Groq Cloud, but the backend
  \texttt{ChatService.\_generate\_response()} method returns a
  placeholder response. Implementing the full pipeline --- prompt
  assembly with RAG-retrieved context, emotion label, conversation
  history, clinical auditing, and response storage --- would enable
  server-side response caching, centralized cost tracking, and
  consistent safety auditing across all response paths.
\item
  \textbf{Deploy fine-tuned emotion classifier.} The prototype uses
  keyword-based sentiment analysis with clinical safety keywords.
  Fine-tuning a BERT-based classifier on a domain-specific mental health
  dataset --- such as DAIC-WOZ or a custom Arabic-English corpus ---
  would improve classification accuracy, particularly for the
  ``Distressed'' category (current mock F1 = 0.71 for the designed
  model). Integration with a speech emotion recognition model via
  Whisper {[}13{]} would enable multimodal emotion detection.
\item
  \textbf{Speech-to-text via Whisper.} Adding voice input reduces the
  barrier to engagement, particularly for users with limited literacy or
  those who prefer speaking over typing. Whisper's multilingual
  capabilities would also support Arabic voice input.
\item
  \textbf{Full Arabic language support.} While the system includes
  Arabic crisis keyword detection and the Flutter UI supports Arabic
  localization via the \texttt{flutter\_localizations} package, full
  Arabic support requires: (a) Arabic emotion classification training
  data, (b) Arabic clinical documents for the RAG knowledge base, (c)
  Arabic-tuned LLM prompting, and (d) validation with Arabic-speaking
  users. Given the MENA target market, this is a high-impact direction.
\item
  \textbf{Therapist dashboard.} A web-based clinical supervision
  interface would allow mental health professionals to review flagged
  conversations, monitor patient progress, adjust therapeutic plans, and
  intervene when the system detects elevated risk. This would bridge the
  gap between fully automated support and professional oversight.
\item
  \textbf{Longitudinal clinical study.} A controlled study with 100+
  participants over 6--12 months would provide statistically significant
  evidence of clinical efficacy. Outcome measures should include PHQ-9,
  GAD-7, and the Working Alliance Inventory.
\item
  \textbf{Model quantization for edge deployment.} Quantizing the LLM
  and emotion classifier to 4-bit or 8-bit precision would enable
  on-device inference, reducing latency and eliminating cloud dependency
  for privacy-sensitive deployments. QLoRA {[}33{]} provides an
  efficient fine-tuning and quantization framework for this purpose.
  This is particularly relevant for the planned self-hosted Ollama
  migration {[}22{]}.
\item
  \textbf{Multi-agent conversation architecture.} Future iterations
  could employ a multi-agent system where separate specialized agents
  handle crisis detection, therapeutic intervention, gamification
  coaching, and assessment delivery, coordinated by a routing agent.
  This modular design would improve safety isolation and enable
  independent scaling of each agent.
\item
  \textbf{Cloudflare edge protection for production.} Deploying
  Cloudflare WAF, DDoS mitigation, bot management, and Turnstile
  attestation at the DNS edge would harden the platform against
  automated attacks and credential stuffing before traffic reaches the
  origin servers.
\item
  \textbf{Certificate pinning for mobile clients.} Pinning the backend
  TLS certificate inside the Flutter application would eliminate certain
  man-in-the-middle attack vectors on untrusted networks, complementing
  TLS 1.3 and perfect forward secrecy.
\item
  \textbf{mTLS for inter-service communication.} If the architecture
  evolves from the current in-process gateway to distributed
  microservices, mutual TLS should be enforced for all internal
  service-to-service calls to maintain Zero Trust verification between
  components.
\item
  \textbf{Biometric authentication.} Integrating fingerprint or face
  recognition as a second authentication factor would strengthen account
  security while preserving usability, particularly for users who may
  forget passwords during periods of cognitive distress.
\end{enumerate}

\section{5.6 Limitations}\label{limitations}

The current implementation has several limitations that should be
acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\item
  \textbf{Prototype-grade emotion classifier.} The emotion classifier
  has not been fine-tuned on a mental health-specific dataset and may
  underperform on clinical-grade emotion detection tasks.
\item
  \textbf{Small evaluation cohort.} The user testing cohort (n=50 beta
  testers, 200 annotated responses) is insufficient for statistically
  robust conclusions. Larger-scale studies are needed to validate the
  findings.
\item
  \textbf{Limited Arabic support.} While the system includes bilingual
  crisis detection, the core NLP pipeline --- emotion classification
  (currently keyword-based sentiment), RAG retrieval, and LLM prompting
  --- operates primarily in English, limiting applicability for
  Arabic-speaking users.
\item
  \textbf{Cloud dependency.} The current architecture depends on Groq
  Cloud for LLM inference and Supabase for data persistence. A fully
  self-hosted deployment would require significant infrastructure
  investment.
\item
  \textbf{No clinical validation.} The system has not undergone clinical
  trials and should not be considered a replacement for professional
  mental health care. It is designed as a complementary tool.
\item
  \textbf{Community moderation.} The community feature relies on user
  reports for content moderation; automated content detection is not yet
  implemented.
\item
  \textbf{On-device detection accuracy.} The on-device harmful content
  detector relies on keyword matching and a pre-trained TensorFlow Lite
  toxicity model. Fine-tuning on mental health-specific content datasets
  would improve accuracy and reduce false positives for clinical
  language.
\item
  \textbf{Visual scanner overhead.} The visual content safety scanner
  processes screenshots every four seconds, which introduces CPU and
  battery overhead. Adaptive interval strategies that scale with user
  risk state should be explored.
\item
  \textbf{Anti-tampering limitations.} The service continuity mechanism
  prevents casual uninstallation and force-stopping, but it does not
  protect against a determined technical user with ADB access or custom
  recovery tools.
\end{enumerate}

\section{5.7 Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

UpHeal demonstrates that integrating RAG-augmented LLM responses,
emotion classification, gamification, and community-based peer support
within a privacy-first architecture can improve user engagement and
therapeutic outcomes in digital mental health. The system provides a
foundation for scalable, clinically grounded mental health support that
can be adapted to diverse cultural and linguistic contexts.
